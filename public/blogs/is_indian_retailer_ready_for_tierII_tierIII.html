Is organized retail ready for Tier II & Tier III cities in India?

By ABHISHEK DADOO
72% of organized retail stores in India are in Tier I cities | 22% in Tier II | And only 7% in Tier III

There is a buzz about evolving shopping trends in Tier II and Tier III cities in India and every organized retailer seems to agree. In a recently published paper by The Associated Chambers of Commerce and Industry of India these trends are attributed to increase in average spending power, demand for various products under one roof, and the increase in brand consciousness amongst the populous in Tier II and Tier III cities. However, are the organized retailers positioned to tap into this burgeoning market? Shoffr analysed data across 132 Indian cities (18 Tier I, 51 Tier 2, 63 Tier III) and 140 brands covering 10,552 stores currently live on its geo-discovery platform.

It turns out that the truth is somewhere in the middle. Different segments of organized retail are at different phases of their Tier II & Tier III journey. While the objective of this article is not to question the expansion strategy of brands and master franchisees in India, it is evident that some segments are missing out on the Tier II & Tier III boom, while some other segments are clearly beating the curve. Our data reveals that on average, organized retail stores in India are distributed as follows: 72% in Tier I, 22% in Tier II, and only 7% in Tier III cities. Taking a deep dive of specific retail segments, we noticed the following trends:

Beating the curve in Tier II & Tier III: KIDS, ENTERTAINMENT, APPAREL, SUPERMARKET, SHOES

These retail segments have a direct impact on the feel-good-on-the-outside factor on an individual. Brand aware parents aspire to avail the best resources for their kids. Hence brands like Giny & Jony and Mom & Me have a disproportionately high distribution of stores in Tier II and Tier III cities. Lack of entertainment opportunities has led to movies theatres becoming a family destination in these cities. Its entertainment, movie choices, and food under one roof after all! As such, PVR, Inox, and Big Cinemas have caught on to the trend ahead of time: so long for single screen cinema? Data also reveals that local tailors might be getting stiff competition from the apparel brands foraying into Tier II & Tier III in a big way.

Below the curve in Tier II & Tier III: FOOD & BEVERAGES, COSMETICS, ELECTRONICS, HOME & LIFESTYLE, HEALTH & SPA, and SPORTS

Feel-good-on-the-inside seems to be another story; both inside the home and personal health related retail segments seem to be finding their foot in Tier II & Tier III cities. Today, this scenario might be attributed to the strong foothold of local electronic stores or the comfort level people have with their local masseuse. However as awareness increases, Electronics, Home & Lifestyle, Health & Spa, and Sport segments have a stellar potential in Tier II & Tier III cities. Branding and marketing will be the mantra for organized retailers in these segments over the next few years.

What’s up with ice-cream parlours and fast food chains? One would have thought they would be the first ones to jump on the Tier II & Tier III opportunity, but our data shows quite the contrary. While organized distribution of perishable goods to Tier II & Tier III cities is a known concern for these merchants, lack of demand due to local tastes might be another contributing factor.

Cosmetics is largely a women’s only segment and wooing the vibrant woman of a Tier II or Tier III city is no mean feat. Shoffr conjectures that demand for cosmetics in Tier II & Tier III cities lags Tier I cities. A Tier I woman has many more social interactions on a day to day basis than her Tier II counterpart and hence cosmetics have a significant share of mind of a metro woman. So how do marketers of cosmetic brands get the share of mind of a Tier II or Tier III woman? Once the answer is clear, there is no doubt that cosmetic brands will also invest heavily in Tier II and Tier III cities.

Note: Data used in this analysis was gathered by Shoffr’s data team via various e- listings websites and directly from organized retailer web portals.
